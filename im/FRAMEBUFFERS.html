What are Framebuffers?

A framebuffer acts as a temporary "canvas" that sits inside of a computer's memory. They are often used so that the program can draw the lines, points and other objects and have then all displayed at once. Without a framebuffer, the drawing on the screen can often have a disconcerting flickering effect.

Using a framebuffer adds one simple step every time you want to display something on the LED Matrix. Usually, that means drawing on the framebuffer takes the following steps:

  • Erase the framebuffer to start with a blank canvas.

  • Draw whatever you want on the framebuffer - lines, rectangles, points, etc.

  • Show your framebuffer on the LED Matrix.

For every LED on the LED Matrix, the framebuffer stores a number, between 0 and 15. This number is the color of the LED - colors are shades of red:

We've already seen the framebuffer point() function. It takes two parameters: x and y, which define which LED to light up. x is the distance in from left to right, starting at zero. y is the distance in from bottom to top, also starting at zero.

We've also already seen the erase(), which erases the framebuffer. erase() can also take a color as a parameter, which will "erase" the screen with that color.

The show() function displays whatever is in the framebuffer on the LED. If you've written some code to draw something on the LED display and it does not appear, it's likely you've forgotten to call show().

A framebuffer can also let you know the width and height of the LED matrix display. This perhaps seems obvious - the width and height are always 8. But, in fact, you can build bigger displays with more than one LED matrix. If you do, the total width and height will be different (larger) than 8.
