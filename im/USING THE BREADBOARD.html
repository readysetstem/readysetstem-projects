USING THE BREADBOARD

In the previous sections, we talked about circuits, how they work, how we build them and how we represent them on a schematic diagram. We even built our first circuit using a battery, a resistor, and LED and some wires.

When it comes to building circuits in real life, connecting wires and components together like we did above – either twisting the wires together or soldering them together – is certainly one way to do it. While sometimes it makes sense to build circuits by twisting wires together or soldering, the problem is that these modifying these types of circuits can be very difficult and cumbersome.

In most cases, you’ll want to build temporary circuits that are easy to modify – this lets you “play around” with the circuit and work out the kinks in your design; then, once you have a temporary circuit that is working exactly the way you want, you can use the design as the basis for your permanent circuit.

One of the most common ways of building a temporary circuit involves the use of a “breadboard,” which you may also hear referred to as a “proto-board.” The name breadboard stems from the fact that early electronics hobbyists would often use the large wooden boards designed for rolling out and cutting bread dough to house their temporary circuits.

The breadboards used to build electronics are often called “solderless” breadboards, which can help avoid confusion when talking to your pastry chef friends. 

Breadboard Design

Breadboards come in many shapes and sizes, but the fundamental components of the breadboard will remain the same:

We’ll get to the purpose of each of these parts in a moment, but the key to a breadboard’s usefulness is how the various areas are electrically connected. Those connections are illustrated here:

As you can see, each of the two rails consists of two long row of connections (a red row in each rail and a blue row in each rail). There are spaces between the groups of holes in the rail, but the entire row is still electrically connected as indicated. Also note that the two red rows are not connected and the two blue rows are not connected – they are denoted in the same color only because they serve the same purpose.

In the center of the board, each row of five connect strips are electrically connected together. While I only highlighted a few rows of connect strips, in actuality, every row of five is a connected strip. It’s important to note that there is no electrical connection across the notch – there’s good reason for this which we’ll get to in a bit.

If you recall from our previous on basic electrical circuits, in order to have a closed circuit, electricity must be able to flow from power, through the circuit, to ground. A breadboard makes it easy to wire a circuit in this fashion – the rails contain the start of the circuit (power) and the end of the circuit (ground), while the connect strips provide space for your components and the connections between those components.

Let’s look back at our example schematic from our first circuit:

To implement that design on our breadboard, we would follow the schematic from power to ground, inserting each component along the way. Let’s look at this step-by-step:

  1. Provide power and ground to the breadboard.

The first step is to provide power and ground to the breadboard. This is done by wiring the (+) terminal of the 3V battery to one of the holes in the red row along the rail. This will provide power to the entire red row, as all the holes in that row are connected.

Next, wire the (-) terminal of the 3V battery to one of the holes in the blue row along the rail. This will provide ground to the entire blue row, as all holes in that row are connected.

Here’s what the breadboard should look like at this point (with the corresponding portion of the schematic):

  1. Bring power to one of the connect strips on the breadboard.

Next, we will connect power from the red rail to one of the connect strips on the breadboard (the connect strips are where we’ll be creating the heart of the circuit). We do this by running a wire from the power row (the red row along the rail) to any connect strip, and firmly planting the ends of the wire into their respective holes. Remember, it doesn’t matter which hole you choose in the power row, as the entire row is connected. And it doesn’t matter which connect strip you choose or which hole of the connect strip you choose.

Here’s what the breadboard should look like at this point (with the corresponding portion of the schematic):

  1. Connect the resistor.

The next step is to connect the resistor to the breadboard. We do this by inserting one end of the resistor into the same connect strip as the wire. We then insert the other end of the resistor into a different connect strip. Because all five holes in the connect strip are connected, there is now continuity all the way from the power source (the battery) to the first connect strip, through the resistor, and to the second connect strip.

Here’s what the breadboard should look like at this point (with the corresponding portion of the schematic):

  1. Provide continuity from the resistor to new connect strip.

We now place a wire from the same connect strip as the resistor to a new connect strip. This new connect strip is where we will connect the first lead of our LED.

Here’s what the breadboard should look like at this point (with the corresponding portion of the schematic):

  1. Connect the LED.

Now that we have continuity from the resistor to a new connect strip, it’s time to insert the next component – the LED. We insert one end of the LED anywhere in the connect strip we just wired. And we insert the other end of the LED into a new connect strip.

Note that LEDs must be oriented the correct way for electricity to flow through them, so ensure that the long side of the LED is the side inserted into second connect strip.

Here’s what the breadboard should look like at this point (with the corresponding portion of the schematic):



  1. Complete the circuit.

We now have continuity all the way from power, through the resistor and to the LED – it’s now time to complete the circuit by attaching the LED to ground. We do this by attaching the second leg of the LED to the blue row along the rail (the ground row). Because the second leg of the LED is embedded in a connect strip, we can accomplish this by connecting any part of that connect strip to any hole in the blue row using a wire.

Now that the circuit is completed, electricity should be flowing, and your LED should be lit!
